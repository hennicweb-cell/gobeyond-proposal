Understanding Go Beyond – mission, context and the recruitment challenge

Go Beyond is a children’s charity with a beautifully clear mission: giving disadvantaged children and young people aged 8 to 13 a free, brilliant week away at residential centres in Cornwall and Derbyshire. Over 30 years, more than 20,000 children have had that experience. The goal now is to welcome more than 1,000 children every year – which means growing, not just sustaining, the income that makes it possible.

Every break is fully funded by voluntary income. Go Beyond raises approximately £1.8 million per year with no government funding – an income model that reflects strong donor relationships but also a concentration risk the board takes seriously. The trusts and foundations team, now four strong and performing well, provides a stable core. The strategic priority is to build an equally structured supporter engagement function around individual giving, regular giving, legacies, challenge events, and major donors.

From our conversation with Sara, a clear picture emerged – and it is worth being direct about what that picture shows.

Go Beyond has tried twice to fill this role. Both candidates came through a standard advertised process, both performed well at interview, and neither delivered. The first had a conflict of interest that went undetected until significant damage was done; the second had ideas but could not translate them into operational plans, and fell apart when a key team member left unexpectedly. In both cases, the problem was not finding candidates. The problem was that job adverts surface people who present well, not necessarily people who perform.

This matters because the role is genuinely demanding. It is not a single discipline. It requires someone who can manage projects across direct mail, digital, database, storytelling, events stewardship and campaign delivery – while keeping one eye permanently on the fundraising return. What Go Beyond needs is a different process to find a different kind of person.

What this appointment actually requires

Having reviewed the job description and spoken in depth with Sara, I want to offer a considered view of the right candidate profile – because I believe it differs somewhat from how the role has been framed in previous advertising.

This is, at its core, a direct marketing role with a fundraising outcome. The candidate does not need to have come from a charity background – but they must understand that everything they do is measured against income return. The most useful frame is someone from a client services or campaign management background in a direct marketing or fundraising agency: comfortable managing multiple workstreams and specialists, ROI-focused by instinct, and experienced in keeping complex projects on track without personally executing every element.

Sara confirmed this resonated strongly. She has tried a candidate from a marketing background who could not engage with the fundraising discipline, and a candidate from a fundraising background who could not handle the breadth of the marketing delivery. What she needs sits in the middle: someone who can do both, and who will not flinch from either.

In practice, the right person will:

  • Project-manage across direct mail, digital, database, PR, events stewardship and annual reporting – knowing when to brief in a freelancer, when to direct the digital officer or database coordinator, and when to do the work themselves
  • Be genuinely comfortable with data – not just running reports, but understanding what the data is saying and using it to make campaign decisions. Raiser’s Edge familiarity is desirable; curiosity about systems is essential
  • Think in terms of supporter journeys and retention – the regular giving programme currently brings in around £90,000 with minimal stewardship. There is significant headroom there for a switched-on direct marketer
  • Be able to translate ideas into operational delivery – both previous candidates had good ideas in interview but could not bridge the gap to a working plan. This is the quality Sara most needs to test for
  • Collaborate easily across a small, close team – campaign ideas will feed into the corporate fundraiser, the trusts team, the major donor function. This person cannot work in a silo
  • Be comfortable with the fundraising imperative – not a pure marketer who considers income targets someone else’s problem

On geography: the role is fully remote, but Sara and I discussed the value of prioritising candidates in the South West and East Midlands – regions that are both rich in direct marketing and charity fundraising talent (Bristol in particular has a strong cluster of charity-sector direct marketing agencies) and practically useful given the locations of Go Beyond’s centres and team members.

A note on job title and candidate attraction

Sara raised the question of job title during our call, and it is worth addressing directly in this proposal. Previous versions of the role have used Supporter Care Manager, Campaigns and Communications, and most recently Supporter Engagement and Fundraising Manager.

My suggestion, and Sara was open to it, is to consider whether the title is currently attracting the right search behaviour from candidates. Someone with a direct marketing or campaign management background is unlikely to be searching for “supporter engagement.” A title such as Fundraising and Marketing Manager, or Direct Marketing Manager (Fundraising), would be more likely to surface relevant candidates through proactive search – while keeping the fundraising imperative front and centre.

We can agree the final title as part of the briefing. The important thing is that the framing is honest: this is a hands-on, delivery-focused, income-driven role in a small charity with big ambitions. That is genuinely compelling to the right candidate. We should say so clearly.

Recommended approach and process

I recommend a bespoke retained search, combining proactive headhunting with a targeted advertised campaign. The process is designed to give Go Beyond confidence that the shortlist has been drawn from the widest and most appropriate talent pool – not just the candidates who happened to apply.

Full briefing

We begin with an in-depth briefing call going well beyond the job description. We agree the functional and behavioural competencies, the cultural fit indicators, the geographic search focus, and how the role and Go Beyond should be positioned to attract candidates who are not actively looking. We also agree the three functional and three behavioural competency questions I will use to assess every candidate I progress.

Talent mapping and proactive outreach

I build a talent pool from the ground up – mapping direct marketers, campaign managers and fundraisers across the sector with relevant experience. Given the profile Sara and I discussed, I will focus particularly on people who have worked within or alongside charity direct marketing agencies, as well as fundraising generalists with strong campaign delivery track records. I will post a targeted advert, but the proactive work is where the best candidates are found.

Weekly transparency

Sara and I meet for approximately 30 minutes each week. She will see the talent pool as it develops – who has been identified, approached and progressed, and who has been ruled out and why. There are no black boxes. This also allows us to retune the search quickly if something is not landing as expected.

Candidate assessment

Every candidate I progress to longlist is competency interviewed by me against the agreed framework. Sara receives a written report on each: strengths, risks and development considerations. Given what happened with the previous two appointments – both of whom performed well in interview but not in role – I will pay particular attention to probing the gap between ideas and delivery: can this person actually show me a plan, not just describe one?

Shortlist and final interview

Sara receives a shortlist of typically three to five candidates, already assessed and briefed. The final interview task – which Sara has used previously to good effect – can be designed around the specific delivery gap the last two appointments exposed. By the time Go Beyond is interviewing, the heavy lifting is done.

A note on major donors

Sara mentioned at the end of our call that major donor development is a separate priority – Go Beyond has significant donors within its existing database (including one giving at the £200,000 level), but currently lacks the dedicated resource to research, cultivate and develop that income line systematically.

She was open to the idea of a freelance or contract arrangement rather than a permanent hire – someone who could come in for three to six months, build the pipeline and methodology, and leave Go Beyond equipped to run with it.

I have access to people who work in exactly this way – experienced major donor practitioners who take on set-piece engagements for smaller charities and deliver real pipeline development rather than just a strategy document. I would be happy to discuss this as a parallel workstream once the Supporter Engagement and Fundraising Manager appointment is underway.
